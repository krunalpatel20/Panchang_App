# PART ONE: THE FOUNDATION

---

## WHAT THIS APP IS NOT

Not Co-Star. Co-Star points inward — at you, your chart, your personality, your compatibility. It is fundamentally self-referential. This app points outward — at time itself, at the quality of a moment in the lunar cycle, at what the tradition says about right now. Co-Star asks: *who are you?* This app asks: *where are you in the year, and what does that mean?*

Not Drik Panchang. Drik Panchang is a data instrument. It tells you that Ekadashi ends at 7:42 PM. This app tells you why that matters, what people have done about it for three thousand years, and what you could do about it today.

Not a religion app. Religion requires belief. This app requires only curiosity. A non-Hindu spouse, a second-generation kid who fasted once because their grandmother asked them to, a person who has never set foot in a temple — all of them are the right user.

---

## THE PROBLEM WE ARE SOLVING

The Atithi Toran was a paper calendar that hung on the wall in Indian homes. It kept track of festivals, auspicious days, fasting days, and the rhythm of the lunar year. Your parents looked at it. They reminded you what today was. They explained, briefly, what to do. You did it, or you didn't, but you knew.

That layer is gone. You live somewhere else. The wall has a different calendar on it. The parent who knew is far away or no longer here. The intercultural household means the tradition now needs justification before it can simply be observed.

This app is the Atithi Toran rebuilt for that household. Not a museum piece. An ambient layer that shows up at the right moment and says: *today matters, here's why, here's what to do.* Gentle. Non-judgmental. Present.

---

## THE VOICE

> A wise friend who loves this world and wants you to feel at home in it.
> Never a priest testing your devotion. Never a professor explaining a textbook.
> Warm, specific, grounded, occasionally poetic. Never vague. Never preachy.

The voice is the product. If the writing is right, everything else follows.

---

## THE FIVE WRITING RULES

**1. Earn the unfamiliar word.**
Sanskrit and Hindi terms are used — not avoided, not over-explained. The meaning arrives through image, not definition.
- ✗ "Ekadashi (the 11th lunar day)"
- ✓ "Ekadashi — the moon's still point, twice a month"

**2. Specific beats vague, always.**
- ✗ "A time for reflection and gratitude"
- ✓ "The day you fast, rest your digestion, and let your mind go quiet"

**3. The emotional beat comes last.**
Every notification ends on something that lands in the body, not the brain.
- ✗ "Observe this auspicious day with family"
- ✓ "You're participating in something four thousand years old"

**4. Bridge the ancient and the observable.**
The tradition was built on observation. Connect what the tradition says to what a person can actually notice or feel — without claiming science confirms it, and without dismissing the tradition as mere superstition.
- ✗ "Science has proven that fasting on Ekadashi..."
- ✗ "It is believed that the moon affects the body..."
- ✓ "The moon on the eleventh tithi creates a specific gravitational pull on water in the body. The tradition noticed this thousands of years before anyone had words for gravitational pull. Whether or not you believe it: try fasting on Ekadashi once and notice what happens."

**5. Always end at human scale.**
A specific action. A person to call. A thing to make. The tradition always had a concrete expression — a food, a ritual, a gesture. End there.
- ✗ "Honor this sacred day"
- ✓ "Make kheer tonight. Leave it on the windowsill. In the morning it will taste like you did something right."

---

## THE ABSOLUTION PARAGRAPH

This lives somewhere in onboarding. It is written once, placed carefully, and never repeated — because repeating it would make it a sales pitch rather than a truth.

> You don't have to have done this perfectly to start now. You don't have to have grown up with it, or observed every fast, or known the Sanskrit words, or had a grandmother who explained things. The tradition survived a lot — centuries of travel, partition, diaspora, intermarriage, forgetting. It can survive you coming to it late. You're here now. That's the beginning.

Each festival has five layers:

| Layer | Format | Length | Where it lives |
|-------|--------|--------|----------------|
| **ADVANCE** | Notification | 2–3 sentences | 7–10 days before |
| **EVE** | Notification | 2–3 sentences | Night before |
| **MORNING** | Notification | 1–2 sentences | Day of, 8am |
| **DEEP DIVE** | In-app text | 5 paragraphs | Tap to open |
| **THE FOOD** | In-app recipe note | 1 paragraph + recipe link | Within deep dive |

The food layer is not optional. Every festival has one. The food is the most transmissible, most sensory, most immediate part of the tradition — and the founder has a food writing background. Use it.

---

## THE MONTHLY HEARTBEAT

The lunar calendar has content every single day. The rhythm is:

**Major festivals** — 15–20 per year. Full treatment: advance, eve, morning, deep dive, food.

**Ekadashi** — 24 times per year, the anchor of the monthly rhythm. Always gets a notification. Named Ekadashis get a longer treatment.

**Purnima (full moon)** — 12–13 times per year. Named Purnimas get their own entry. Every Purnima gets a notification.

**Amavasya (new moon)** — 12–13 times per year. Always ancestral in tone. Always gets a notification.

**Paksha transitions** — the moment Shukla Paksha becomes Krishna Paksha, and vice versa. Short notification. The app marks these because the tradition does. No other app does.

**Named tithis** — Chaturthi (Ganesha, twice monthly), Pradosh (Shiva, twice monthly), Ashtami (Durga, twice monthly). Not every one gets a notification, but the significant ones do.

This is 30 days of content per month. Not just festival days. That is what makes this a daily habit rather than a seasonal check-in.

## THE ORAL TRADITION CONSIDERATION

Everything in this document assumes a reading user. But this knowledge traveled orally for most of its existence. Your grandmother didn't read about Ekadashi — she was told about it while the sabudana was already soaking.

**V2 direction:** A morning audio layer. Sixty seconds. Warm voice. What today is and why it matters. This is more intimate than text. It maps to how the knowledge actually lived. Consider it the second version of the notification — for users who want to hear it rather than read it.

## 🌅 MAKAR SANKRANTI
*~January 14 | Solar transition — sun enters Capricorn*
*The only major Hindu festival on the solar calendar. Same date almost every year.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Makar Sankranti is in a week — the day the sun crosses into Capricorn, and one of the few Hindu festivals tied to the solar calendar, which means it falls on the same date almost every year. In Gujarat, the sky fills with kites. Til-gul gets made and shared. The phrase goes: *til-gul ghya, god god bola* — take sesame and jaggery, speak sweetly. There's a whole philosophy in that one line.

**EVE**
> Tomorrow: the astronomical new year in many traditions. The nights stop getting longer. The sun begins its return. Prepare some til-gul if you can — sesame and jaggery rolled together, one of the oldest sweets in the subcontinent. There's a reason people have been making it on this day for thousands of years.

**MORNING**
> The sun crosses into Capricorn today. In Gujarat, the sky is full of kites right now. Go outside at some point and look up.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Makar Sankranti is a solar festival — *Sankranti* means transition, *Makara* is Capricorn. The sun crosses into Capricorn on this day, beginning its six-month northern journey called *Uttarayana*. Unlike almost every other Hindu festival, which follows the lunar calendar and shifts dates each year, Sankranti is anchored to the sun. It falls on January 14th almost every year. That consistency made it an anchor point for agricultural communities before anyone had a printed calendar.

*The mythology:* In the Mahabharata, Bhishma Pitamaha — the great patriarch, lying on a bed of arrows after being mortally wounded — chose to wait for Uttarayana before allowing himself to die. He lay there for weeks, keeping himself alive through will. When the sun finally turned north, he let go. The tradition reads this as: Uttarayana is when the gates between worlds are more open. Even the dying choose this direction.

*The history:* Uttarayana has been observed across South Asia for at least three thousand years. The festival absorbed regional variations — Pongal in the South (a four-day harvest festival where the first rice of the season is cooked outdoors until it boils over, the overflow auspicious), Lohri in Punjab (a bonfire festival the night before), Bihu in Assam. Different rituals, the same sun.

*The regional variation:* In Gujarat, this is kite day — *Uttarayan* is what Gujaratis call it, and it's taken with full competitive seriousness. People spend days preparing kites, stay up all night flying them by searchlight, and compete from rooftops with manja (glass-coated string) designed to cut the opponent's line. The sky literally changes color. There are injuries. There is also tremendous communal joy.

*What to do today:* Make or find til-gul — sesame seeds and jaggery pressed into rounds. The sesame is warming and seasonal; jaggery is unrefined, honest sweetness. Give some to someone you've been meaning to reconnect with. Say the words: *til-gul ghya, god god bola.* Take sweetness. Speak sweetly. Then do it.

**THE FOOD**
Til-gul ladoo: equal parts white sesame seeds (toasted until golden) and powdered jaggery, mixed warm and rolled into small rounds. Some families add dry coconut or cardamom. Underdone and they fall apart; overdone and they harden. The right consistency comes from making them a few times. Ask someone who knows.

## 🌸 VASANT PANCHAMI
*~Late January/February | Shukla Panchami of Magha*
*Saraswati's day. The official arrival of spring.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Vasant Panchami is coming — the fifth day of the bright fortnight in Magha, and the official arrival of spring in the Hindu calendar. Saraswati's day. Goddess of knowledge, music, language, and the arts. If there's something you've been wanting to learn, or a creative project that's been sitting untouched — this is when you begin it.

**EVE**
> Tomorrow is Saraswati Puja. The tradition: place books, instruments, pens — whatever tools you work with — at the feet of her image overnight. If that's not your practice, the quieter version is: write down one thing you want to understand more deeply this year. That's an offering too.

**MORNING**
> Vasant Panchami. Spring has arrived by the Hindu calendar, even if the weather disagrees. Wear yellow today if you can — mustard flowers are blooming in the fields of North India right now. Same flowers, different latitudes.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Vasant means spring. Panchami means fifth day. This is the fifth day of the bright lunar fortnight in Magha — a seasonal marker and a goddess's day simultaneously. It announces that winter's hold is breaking, and it arrives earlier than any other spring marker on the calendar.

*The mythology:* Saraswati is one of the three great goddesses — alongside Lakshmi (abundance) and Durga (power). She carries a veena, a book, prayer beads, and a lotus. She presides over *Vak* — speech itself — which is why she is goddess not just of music and art but of language, learning, and all knowledge that takes form in words. In the Rigveda, one of the oldest texts in continuous use anywhere, there is a hymn to Saraswati as a river — sacred water that makes things flow and flourish. The river may have been real once. The goddess is older.

*The history:* In Bengal, Saraswati Puja is enormous — colleges shut down, elaborate pandals are constructed, and the day carries the weight of a public holiday built around the idea that learning is worth celebrating publicly. Students place their textbooks before her image the night before exams. Musicians place their instruments. The gesture is less supplication and more humility: what you know, you didn't arrive at alone.

*The regional variation:* Yellow is worn everywhere on this day — the color of the mustard flowers in bloom across North India at exactly this time. In some communities, children begin their education on Vasant Panchami — the first letter written in rice or sand, guided by a teacher or parent. *Vidyarambham* — the beginning of learning — is considered particularly auspicious on this day.

*What to do today:* Begin something. The tradition is specific about this: Vasant Panchami is for beginnings. A new practice, a skill you've been postponing, a project you keep not starting. Put your tools out tonight. Start tomorrow.

**THE FOOD**
Yellow sweets — kesar halwa, boondi ladoo tinted with saffron, or simply anything golden. In some families, yellow rice is made. The yellow is not decoration; it is the color of the day, worn and eaten simultaneously.

## 🌙 MAHA SHIVARATRI
*~February/March | Krishna Chaturdashi of Magha or Phalguna*
*The one festival in the Hindu calendar that doesn't ask you to be happy.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Maha Shivaratri is in a week — the Great Night of Shiva. Not a celebration exactly. More like a vigil. The tradition is to stay awake through the night, fast through the day, and sit with whatever is dark and difficult rather than avoiding it. There are many festivals in the Hindu calendar. This is the one that doesn't ask you to be happy.

**EVE**
> Tonight into tomorrow: Maha Shivaratri. Shiva is most accessible in the darkness, in the quiet, in the parts of yourself you usually move too fast to feel. If you observe it: fast today, stay up as long as you can tonight. If you don't: it's still worth asking what you've been avoiding.

**MORNING**
> Maha Shivaratri. The great night is already half over for those who kept it. Shiva holds the poison that would have destroyed the world — keeps it in his throat forever so others are spared. What are you holding so others don't have to?

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Most Hindu festivals are celebrations. Maha Shivaratri is a vigil. The name means Great Night of Shiva — *Maha* (great), *Shiva* (the deity), *Ratri* (night). It falls on the fourteenth day of the dark fortnight in Magha or Phalguna, just before the new moon. The darkest part of the darkest night of the year, almost. The timing is not accidental.

*The mythology:* When the cosmic ocean was churned by gods and demons in search of the nectar of immortality, a poison also emerged — *Halahala* — so lethal it threatened all of creation. No one would touch it. Shiva drank it, held it in his throat — neither swallowing nor expelling. His throat turned blue. He stayed awake all night to keep the poison from reaching his stomach. The gods stayed with him, singing and praying to keep him alert. That is the vigil re-enacted every year.

*The history:* Shiva is among the most ancient deities in continuous worship anywhere on Earth. Figures that resemble him appear in Indus Valley seals from 2500 BCE — seated in a meditation posture, surrounded by animals. The Shivalinga, his aniconic form, is the most widely worshipped object in the Hindu tradition. He exists before the mythology that grew around him.

*The regional variation:* In Varanasi and all major Shiva pilgrimage sites, the temples stay open and full through the entire night. Devotees pour milk, water, honey, and bel patra leaves over the Shivalinga in a continuous *abhishek* (ritual bathing). In households, the fast is observed and the evening puja is extended. The quieter domestic version is simply: stay up past midnight. Sit with the dark.

*What to do today:* Fast if you observe it. If you don't fast: eat one simple meal. Stay up past midnight — not watching something, just sitting. Shiva is associated with what is untamed, unpolished, unresolved in us. The vigil is for that. Bring bel patra to a temple if there's one near you.

**THE FOOD**
Maha Shivaratri fasting food: fruit, milk, and specific grains — *kuttu* (buckwheat) and *sabudana* (tapioca). No regular grains, no onion, no garlic. The fast breaks at dawn on Chaturdashi with panchamrit — milk, curd, honey, sugar, ghee — offered to Shiva and then consumed.

## 🎨 HOLI
*~March | Purnima of Phalguna*
*Two nights. Most people only know one.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Holi is in a week. The colors, yes — but underneath the colors, a bonfire. The night before is Holika Dahan: a fire lit to burn away everything that should not have survived the winter. The morning is permission to be ridiculous, covered in color, five years old again. Both halves matter.

**EVE — HOLIKA DAHAN**
> Tonight is Holika Dahan — Holi's quieter, older half. A bonfire lit to mark the death of Holika, the demoness who could not be burned, until she tried to burn an innocent. Tonight is for the release. Tomorrow is for the colors.

**MORNING**
> Rang Barse. The colors are out. Go find them.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Holi is two festivals running on consecutive days. Holika Dahan — the bonfire night — is the older ritual. The color festival the next morning is the one most people know. Understanding both changes what the day means.

*The mythology:* Hiranyakashipu, a king who had made himself nearly indestructible, wanted his son Prahlad to worship him rather than Vishnu. Prahlad refused. Hiranyakashipu enlisted his sister Holika — who had a shawl making her immune to fire — to sit in a pyre holding Prahlad in her lap. Devotion protected Prahlad; the shawl flew onto Holika. She burned. He walked out unharmed. The bonfire is lit every year to mark this. Into it, people throw old things — grain, coconut, the previous year's grudges. What should not survive the season goes into that fire.

*The history:* The color tradition comes partly from spring — the powders historically derived from flowers in bloom, from the festival arriving just as the countryside erupted in color. In Vrindavan and Mathura, where Krishna's childhood stories are set, Holi continues for days. Krishna and Radha are woven through the mythology of color — the divine lover whose teasing and play became the model for something far larger than romance.

*The regional variation:* In Vrindavan, Holi is a week-long event. In Bengal, it's *Dol Jatra* — Radha and Krishna's images are placed on decorated swings and carried in procession while colors are thrown. In Gujarat and Maharashtra, the color festival is one morning, vigorous and communal, followed by family meals. Everywhere: the social hierarchy temporarily dissolves. On Holi, nobody is senior or junior. Everyone is covered in the same colors.

*What to do today:* Holika Dahan: write one thing you want to release on a piece of paper and burn it in a candle. The morning: find the colors. Wear something you don't mind ruining. This is the one festival where showing up with no knowledge of the tradition and playing is a completely legitimate form of participation.

**THE FOOD**
Thandai — milk, almonds, fennel seeds, rose petals, cardamom, saffron, black pepper, blended cold. In some regions, an additional ingredient appears in certain households. Gujiya — sweet dumplings filled with khoya and dry fruits, fried and dusted with sugar. The colors get on the food. This is fine.

## 🐘 GANESH CHATURTHI
*~August/September | Shukla Chaturthi of Bhadrapada | 10 days*
*He arrives. On the last day, he dissolves back into the water.*

**ADVANCE (10 days out)**
> Ganesh Chaturthi begins in ten days. For ten days, Ganesha comes home. In Mumbai, this means the city transforms — pandals on every block, processions, music until midnight. Everywhere else, it means something quieter but the same: an invitation to begin again. He is invoked before every new venture. This is his season.

**ADVANCE (3 days out)**
> Three days to Ganesh Chaturthi. What's worth knowing if you're new to it: Ganesha has an elephant head because the original was destroyed and his father Shiva replaced it with the first one he found. He rides a mouse — the ego, which he has learned to carry rather than be carried by. He broke one of his own tusks to write the Mahabharata when the ink ran out. He is the remover of obstacles and the most beloved deity in the tradition. He is almost impossible not to love.

**MORNING**
> Ganpati Bappa Morya. He's here. Welcome him.

**VISARJAN (Day 10)**
> Today he goes back. The clay Ganesha that has lived in the home or the pandal for ten days is carried to the water — river, ocean, lake — and immersed. He dissolves. The chant: *Ganpati Bappa Morya, Pudcha Varshi Laukar Ya* — come back soon next year. That chant, carrying something beloved toward the water, knowing you are choosing to let it go, is one of the most moving things in the Hindu calendar.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Ganesh Chaturthi is a ten-day festival honoring Ganesha — the elephant-headed son of Shiva and Parvati, god of beginnings and remover of obstacles. It begins on *Shukla Chaturthi* (the fourth day of the bright fortnight) in the lunar month of Bhadrapada, which falls in August or September.

*The mythology:* Ganesha's elephant head comes from an act of destruction and restoration. Parvati created him from the sandalwood paste on her body while Shiva was away, and he stood guard at her bath. Shiva, not recognizing him, cut off his head. Parvati's grief was absolute. Shiva sent his attendants to bring back the head of the first living creature they found sleeping with its head pointing north. They found an elephant. Ganesha was restored. He is *Vighnaharta* — the remover of obstacles — and also *Vighnakarta* — the creator of obstacles for those on the wrong path. Both roles matter.

*The history:* The domestic ritual is ancient. The enormous public version was designed in 1893 by Bal Gangadhar Tilak, the Indian independence leader, as political organizing against British colonial rule. The British had banned gatherings of more than a few people. Religious festivals were exempt. Tilak used this to bring tens of thousands together — and in doing so, gave the independence movement a common language that crossed caste and class. The festival he designed still runs in Mumbai exactly as he intended.

*The regional variation:* Maharashtra is the center — Pune's Kasba Ganpati, Mumbai's Lalbaugcha Raja, are not just religious events but city-scale phenomena. In Andhra Pradesh and Karnataka, the festival is similarly large. In Gujarat, it's observed more domestically but with genuine devotion. The ten-day duration is the traditional form; some families observe one and a half days or five days based on tradition.

*What to do today:* If your family installs a Ganesha murti, do the preparation tonight — clean the space, arrange flowers, make modak. If it's just you: identify one obstacle you've been working around instead of removing. That's the offering.

**THE FOOD**
Modak — Ganesha's favorite. A steamed dumpling with a rice flour shell, filled with fresh coconut and jaggery, pinched into a topknot shape at the top. Difficult to make well. Worth attempting once. The fried version (ukadiche modak) is more forgiving. Puran poli is also traditional — a flatbread stuffed with sweet dal and jaggery.

## 🧵 RAKSHA BANDHAN
*~July/August | Purnima of Shravana*
*A thread tied from one person to another as a formalization of care.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Rakhi is in a week. The thread isn't really about siblings — it's about choosing who you'd protect and who you trust to protect you. That circle is worth thinking about. If you have a sibling far away, now is when to mail something.

**EVE**
> Tomorrow: Raksha Bandhan. Historically, women tied rakhis to kings they wanted protection from, to neighbors, to friends. The sibling framing came later. The question underneath it is older: who would you tie a thread to today?

**MORNING**
> Today your wrist becomes a promise. Wherever you are — same city or different continent — the bond the thread represents doesn't need proximity to be real.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Raksha Bandhan — *raksha* meaning protection, *bandhan* meaning bond. A sister ties a rakhi (a thread or decorated bracelet) on her brother's wrist; he gives a gift and promises to protect her. It is genuinely tender, and many people's favorite festival for exactly that reason.

*The mythology:* In the Mahabharata, Draupadi tears a strip from her sari to bind Krishna's wrist when he cuts his finger. He considers himself bound to her from that day — which is why, at the moment of her humiliation in the Kaurava court, he intervenes. In medieval history, Rani Karnavati of Chittor sent a rakhi to the Mughal Emperor Humayun when her kingdom was threatened. He rode to her defense. The thread creates an obligation that crosses ordinary lines.

*The history:* The sibling-specific framing of Raksha Bandhan is a relatively modern consolidation of older, broader practices. The tradition of tying a protective thread — a *raksha* — appears in many forms across Hindu practice. The specificity of sister-to-brother came to dominate, but it was never the whole story.

*The regional variation:* In coastal Maharashtra, Raksha Bandhan coincides with *Narali Purnima* — the coconut full moon — when fishermen offer coconuts to the sea marking the end of the monsoon and the reopening of fishing season. The full moon of Shravana carries multiple meanings depending on where you are.

*What to do today:* Call your sibling. If you have no siblings: call whoever you'd protect. If they're far: mail the thread. The gesture doesn't require proximity. It requires the intention to say: I see you, I've got you, I'm here.

**THE FOOD**
Whatever sweets your family makes together. There is no canonical Raksha Bandhan food — it's the meal after the tying that matters, and what's on the table depends entirely on who you're with.

## 🎶 JANMASHTAMI
*~August | Krishna Ashtami of Shravana Krishna Paksha*
*Born at midnight. In a prison. During a storm.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Krishna's birthday is in a week. He was born at midnight — in a prison cell, during a storm, to parents who had already had six children killed by the king who imprisoned them. The gods arranged everything: the guards fell asleep, the locks opened, the river parted. The most beloved figure in the Hindu tradition entered the world in the most unlikely circumstances. That's worth sitting with.

**EVE**
> Tonight into tomorrow: Janmashtami. The fast holds until midnight, when Krishna is born. At midnight, bells ring, conches blow, and the fast breaks with *panchamrit* — milk, curd, honey, sugar, and ghee. If you're not fasting: stay up until midnight anyway. There's something about marking the exact moment of a birth, four thousand years after it happened, that doesn't require belief to be meaningful.

**MIDNIGHT NOTIFICATION**
> He's here. *Nand Gher Anand Bhayo, Jai Kanhaiya Lal Ki.* Born at midnight in a prison, and the whole cosmos celebrated. Happy Janmashtami.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Janmashtami — the birthday of Krishna. It falls on the eighth day (*Ashtami*) of the dark fortnight in Shravana. The midnight timing is not symbolic — it is the actual birth time, embedded in the mythology, and the observance is structured around it: the fast breaks at midnight, which is when the celebration begins.

*The mythology:* Krishna is the most multidimensional figure in the Hindu tradition. He is simultaneously: the butter-stealing child of Vrindavan; the flute-playing young lover whose music pulled the universe toward him; the charioteer-strategist of the Kurukshetra war; and the cosmic form in Chapter 11 of the Bhagavad Gita — infinite, consuming armies in his mouth like rivers flowing into the sea. No other deity contains this range. The child Krishna and the Gita Krishna seem almost to be different beings. That's part of the point.

*The history:* The Bhagavad Gita — eighteen chapters of philosophy delivered on a battlefield — is one of the most read texts in human history. Its central argument: act without attachment to results. Fulfill your duty without calculating personal gain. The self that dies is not the self that matters. These ideas influenced Emerson, Thoreau, Gandhi, and Oppenheimer (who quoted it at the Trinity nuclear test: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds"). All of that comes from the charioteer born at midnight in a prison in Mathura.

*The regional variation:* Mathura and Vrindavan observe Janmashtami for days — it is a city-sized event. In Maharashtra, *Dahi Handi* is the physical tradition: a pot of curd is hung high above the street, and teams of young men build human pyramids to break it, re-enacting Krishna's childhood butter theft. The competition is genuine. In South India, women draw footprints leading from the front door to the kitchen, welcoming Krishna into the home.

*What to do today:* Fast through the day. At midnight, break it with panchamrit. If you have children: tell them the story of the midnight birth — the storm, the prison, the river that parted. This is the best bedtime story in the tradition. Save it for tonight.

**THE FOOD**
Panchamrit: milk, curd, honey, sugar, and ghee mixed together — the sacred five-ingredient offering and the food that breaks the midnight fast. Makhan (fresh white butter) is placed before Krishna's image — the thing the child stole, returned as offering. Panjiri — a dry sweet of wheat flour, ghee, sugar, and dry fruits — is traditional fasting food.

## 🌙✨ NAVRATRI
*~September/October | Shukla Pratipada of Ashwin | 9 nights*
*Nine nights of the goddess. The circle that never stops.*

**ADVANCE (10 days out)**
> Nine nights begin soon. Navratri — the festival of Durga across her nine forms. In Gujarat, it means garba: nine nights of dancing, a whole community moving together in the dark. In other traditions, it means fasting, puja, the clay pot with a flame inside it. Everywhere: the goddess is awake and present. Pay attention.

**NIGHT 1**
> Navratri begins tonight. Shailaputri — the first form. Daughter of the mountains. She carries a trident and a lotus, rides a bull. She is the beginning of strength, before it's been tested. Light a diya.

**NIGHT 5**
> Five nights in. You're at the midpoint of Navratri. The goddess in her fifth form tonight is Skandamata — the mother of the war god, who holds her fierce warrior-son in her lap with perfect tenderness. Power and care in the same hands. If you're dancing garba: the circles are tighter now. Everyone knows the steps.

**NIGHT 9 (NAVAMI)**
> The last night. Tomorrow Ravana burns. But tonight is still hers. Nine nights, nine forms, nine aspects of a power that exists in every person who has ever had to fight for something that mattered. *Jai Mata Di.*

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Navratri means nine nights. It happens four times a year, but Sharad Navratri — the autumn one, in Ashwin — is the one people mean when they say Navratri. This is Durga's festival, arriving after the monsoon ends, when the air changes and the harvest begins.

*The mythology:* Mahishasura, a buffalo-demon, had acquired a boon making him invincible to any man or god. He defeated the gods one by one and took the heavens. In their defeat, the gods pooled every divine energy — each one sending their power outward — and from that pooled light, a woman emerged. Armed with each god's weapon, riding a lion. Durga. She fought Mahishasura for nine nights. On the tenth day, she killed him. The nine nights of Navratri replay that battle. The tenth day is Dussehra — the day of victory.

*The history:* Durga represents *Shakti* — the fundamental energy that sustains the cosmos, which the tradition says is feminine. Not nurturing-feminine or gentle-feminine. Warrior-feminine. The absolute creative-destructive force. Her nine forms (*Navadurga*) each represent a different aspect of that force: Shailaputri (the foundation), Brahmacharini (the ascetic), Chandraghanta (clarity), Kushmanda (creation), Skandamata (the fierce mother), Katyayani (the warrior), Kalaratri (the dark night), Mahagauri (the pure), Siddhidatri (the bestower). Nine faces of the same fire.

*The regional variation:* In Gujarat and Rajasthan, Navratri is garba — concentric circles of dancers spinning around a central lamp or Durga image, hours long, nine nights in a row. The music builds. By the last night, nobody wants to stop. In West Bengal, it's Durga Puja — five days, elaborate clay Durga idols that are works of art, pandals that are architectural projects, and a public celebration unlike anything else in India. On the last day, the idols go into the river. Women apply sindoor to Durga's forehead and to each other's. It is devastating and joyful simultaneously.

*What to do today:* If you're near a garba — go, even if you don't know the steps. The circle doesn't require expertise. If you're fasting: Navratri fasting is *sattvic* — fruits, dairy, buckwheat, root vegetables. Light and clean. On the ninth night: stay up as long as you can.

**THE FOOD**
Navratri fasting food is its own cuisine: sabudana khichdi (tapioca pearls with peanuts, cumin, and green chili), kuttu ki puri (buckwheat flatbread), makhana kheer (lotus seed pudding), samak rice with yogurt. The constraint produces creativity. The fast-breaking meal on Dashami is always a feast.

## 💥 DUSSEHRA (VIJAYADASHAMI)
*~October | Shukla Dashami of Ashwin | Day after Navratri*
*The tenth day. The effigy burns. Something ends.*

**ADVANCE (3 days out)**
> Dussehra in three days — the tenth day, Vijayadashami, the day of victory. Ravana's effigy goes up in cities across India. On Dussehra, it burns. The mythology is clear: arrogance, however magnificent, has a last day.

**EVE**
> Tomorrow Ravana burns. He is a complicated figure to burn — the most learned man in the mythology, master of the Vedas, a devoted son, an able ruler. His flaw was not stupidity. It was the certainty that his power exempted him from consequences. That certainty is the thing in the fire.

**MORNING**
> Vijayadashami. Good has defeated evil, which means tomorrow the work begins again. Ram returns home from fourteen years of exile and war. In ten days: Diwali.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Dussehra — *dasha* (ten) *hara* (defeat). The tenth day of Navratri, commemorating two simultaneous victories: Ram's defeat of Ravana, and Durga's final defeat of Mahishasura. The same solar day carries both.

*The mythology:* Ravana is one of the most interesting antagonists in world literature. He is not simply evil — he is a great king (Lanka was magnificent), a scholar of the highest order (fluent in the Vedas), a devoted son, and a gifted musician who played the veena. His sin is not ignorance. It is pride — the conviction that his learning and power exempt him from the law that applies to everyone else. When he abducts Sita, he knows it is wrong. He chooses his desire over his dharma. The knowledge and the transgression coexist in him.

*The history:* In North India, Ramlila — the theatrical retelling of the Ramayana — runs for the nine days of Navratri and culminates on Dussehra with the burning of enormous effigies. In some cities, the Ravana effigy is ten stories tall, stuffed with fireworks. When it burns, the sky erupts. This is intentional: a public burning of what Ravana represents, large enough that no one can miss it.

*The regional variation:* In the South, this day is *Ayudha Puja* — the worship of tools and instruments of one's trade. Mechanics worship their tools. Musicians worship their instruments. Farmers worship their plows. The principle: whatever helps you do your work is sacred. In Kerala, the day before is *Vidyarambham* — children begin their education, guided by a teacher who moves their hand to write the first letter in sand or rice.

*What to do today:* Name what you're burning. Ravana is not a character from a story — he is the version of an educated, capable person who chose pride over restraint. Identify the version of that in yourself. That's what goes into the fire tonight.

**THE FOOD**
In North India: jalebi and fafda are the traditional Dussehra morning foods in Gujarat — the sweet crisp spiral and the spiced chickpea flatbread, eaten together at breakfast while the air is still cool. The combination is one of the great inexplicable food pairings of the tradition.

## 🪔 DIWALI (DEEPAWALI)
*~October/November | Amavasya of Kartika | 5 days*
*The darkest night. Someone came home. The whole world lit up.*

**DAY 1 — DHANTERAS (2 days before)**
> Diwali has begun. Today is Dhanteras — *dhan* meaning wealth, *teras* meaning the thirteenth day. The tradition: buy something metal for the home today. The deeper version: what are you welcoming in this year? The answer to that question is what you're actually acquiring.

**DAY 2 — CHOTI DIWALI (Eve)**
> The second day. In the South, this is the main celebration. The mythology: Krishna defeated Narakasura before dawn and returned home covered in blood. Satyabhama cleaned him with an oil bath. That ritual — the bath before the light — is still observed. Tonight: prepare the home. The guests arrive tomorrow.

**DAY 3 — DIWALI**
> Tonight is the night. Lakshmi moves through clean, lit homes. Every lamp is an invitation. Every diya on a windowsill is a signal: *we are here, we are home, you are welcome.* The story underneath all the stories is the same — someone came back from somewhere very far away, and the whole world lit up to welcome them. Who are you lighting a lamp for tonight?

**DAY 4 — GUJARATI NEW YEAR (Bestu Varas)**
> In Gujarat: today is the new year. *Saal Mubarak.* The Vikram Samvat turns — not in January, not at the spring equinox, but the morning after Diwali, when the darkness is over and the light has held. The year begins at its quietest point. What are you beginning?

**DAY 5 — BHAI DOOJ**
> The last day: Bhai Dooj. Sisters put a tika on their brothers' foreheads and pray for their long life. The direction reverses from Raksha Bandhan — today the sister offers the protection. Five days end here. *Saal Mubarak.*

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Diwali is five festivals running consecutively, each with its own name, mythology, and regional character. The word *Deepawali* means row of lamps — *deep* (lamp) *avali* (row). But the light is not decorative. In every story that attaches to Diwali, the darkness is real, and the return of light is hard-won.

*The mythology:* The North Indian story: Ram returns to Ayodhya after fourteen years of exile and the defeat of Ravana. The kingdom lit lamps across the city so he could find his way home through the dark night. Every lamp lit on Diwali is that lamp — a welcome home, a signal to whoever is far away that there is a place for them. The South Indian story: Krishna defeated Narakasura, who had imprisoned sixteen thousand women, at dawn on Chaturdashi. The oil bath before sunrise re-enacts Satyabhama cleaning Krishna after the battle. The merchant's story: Lakshmi visits clean, lit, welcoming homes on the new moon night. The cleaning before Diwali is not aesthetic preparation — it is an invitation.

*The history:* Diwali crosses religious lines more than almost any Hindu festival — it is observed by Jains (marking Mahavira's enlightenment), Sikhs (marking the release of Guru Hargobind from prison), and some Buddhists. The festival of light transcends its mythological frames.

*The regional variation:* Gujarati New Year (*Bestu Varas*) falls on the fourth day — the Vikram Samvat year begins not in spring but the morning after Diwali. The year turns at its darkest point, at the new moon after the harvest. That timing is worth noticing: not at the height of abundance but at the moment of completion, when everything has been given and the ground rests.

*What to do today:* Clean your home before Diwali — this matters. Light something after sunset. Make or find a sweet. Call someone you haven't spoken to in too long. If you have children: tell them that someone came home from very far away, and the whole world lit up because they were finally home. Start with that. The rest follows.

**THE FOOD**
Diwali sweets are the most elaborate in the Hindu calendar — a week of preparation, family recipes that don't exist in any cookbook. In Gujarat: mohanthal (besan fudge with ghee and cardamom), chevdo (spiced flattened rice), chakli (spiral rice flour snacks), mathia (spiced crackers). The mithai box that travels between households in the five days of Diwali is its own form of correspondence — what you send says something about how seriously you take the relationship.

## 🔁 EKADASHI (MONTHLY)
*Every 11th tithi of both Shukla and Krishna Paksha — 24 times a year*
*The heartbeat of the app. The anchor of the monthly rhythm.*

---

### WHY EKADASHI IS THE MOST IMPORTANT CONTENT IN THIS APP

Ekadashi happens 24 times a year. Every single time, someone opens the app and receives a version of the same message, slightly different, with the same underlying meaning. That repetition builds the habit. Diwali once a year is an event. Ekadashi twice a month is a rhythm. The rhythm is what makes the app essential rather than seasonal.

The simple explanation — the eleventh day, why it matters, what to do — is not a limitation. It is the heartbeat.

---

### THE "WHY ELEVEN" ANSWER

This is the content nobody else is writing, and it is your most important editorial territory.

The moon on the eleventh tithi creates a specific gravitational pull on the water in the body. The tradition observed this thousands of years before anyone had language for gravitational pull. The fasting on Ekadashi appears in the texts as devotional — an offering to Vishnu — but the framing that has sustained it across millennia is practical: people who fasted on Ekadashi reported that their bodies and minds were clearer. That the quiet arrived easier. That the digestion rested, and the rest extended upward.

Whether or not the gravitational mechanism is the correct explanation: try fasting on Ekadashi once and notice what happens. The tradition built on observation before it built on belief.

This bridge — between what the tradition says and what a person can physically experience — is the editorial voice that distinguishes this app from everything else in this space.

---

### EKADASHI NOTIFICATION TEMPLATES

**STANDARD EKADASHI — EVE**
> Ekadashi tomorrow — the moon at its still point, twice a month. Fast if you observe it. If not: eat lighter than usual, sleep earlier. The quiet that arrives when you stop for a day is its own kind of answer.

**STANDARD EKADASHI — MORNING**
> Ekadashi. The eleventh day. The body knows what to do with a day of rest.

**NIRJALA EKADASHI (June — the most austere)**
> Tomorrow is Nirjala Ekadashi — the one Ekadashi where the fast includes water. No food, no water, from sunrise to the next sunrise. It is considered the most powerful fast of the year: one day of this is said to carry the merit of all twenty-four Ekadashis combined. Most people don't do the full nirjala. That's fine. Even a partial fast today carries the intention. This one has been observed continuously, without interruption, for thousands of years.

**DEVUTTHANA EKADASHI (November — Vishnu wakes)**
> Devutthana Ekadashi tomorrow — the day Vishnu wakes from his four-month cosmic sleep. *Chaturmas* ends. Weddings resume. The auspicious season opens. If something in your life has been on hold waiting for the right moment: this is the moment the tradition designates for beginnings.

**VAIKUNTA EKADASHI (December — the open gate)**
> Tonight is Vaikunta Ekadashi — the most sacred Ekadashi on the Vaishnava calendar. Vishnu is said to open the gates of Vaikuntha on this night, and those who stay awake in prayer pass through them. Temples stay open all night. If you're near a Vishnu or Venkateswara temple: go tonight. The vigil is worth the loss of sleep.

**AMALAKI EKADASHI (March — the amla fast)**
> Amalaki Ekadashi tomorrow. The amla tree — Indian gooseberry — is sacred to Vishnu, and this Ekadashi is specifically associated with it. If you can find amla: eat it, or make amla juice. One of the most nutritionally dense foods in the subcontinent, revered in Ayurveda for as long as Ayurveda has existed. The devotion and the practical overlap completely on this day.

**DEEP DIVE (standard Ekadashi)**

*What it actually is:* Ekadashi means "eleven" in Sanskrit — the eleventh tithi of both the waxing and waning lunar fortnights. It arrives 24 times a year, every month, reliably. It is the most frequently observed fast in the Hindu calendar and possibly the most interesting one to examine.

*The mythology:* In the Vaishnava tradition, Ekadashi is primarily associated with Vishnu — the preserver, the sustainer. The mythology says that on the eleventh tithi, the demon Mura was asleep, guarding the cosmic realm, and a goddess emerged from Vishnu's body and defeated him. This goddess, Ekadashi herself, asked Vishnu for a boon: that those who fast on her day would receive liberation. He granted it. Every Ekadashi is her day.

*The history:* The 24 Ekadashis each have a name and a specific mythology. The full cycle tells a story of the year through Vishnu's avatars and cosmic events. Some are widely observed (Nirjala, Devutthana, Vaikunta), some are regionally specific, some are known only within particular Vaishnava lineages. The tradition is both universal and infinitely detailed.

*The why-eleven question:* The moon on the eleventh tithi is in a specific relationship to the sun — roughly a quarter turn away from either new or full. The gravitational effect on water in the body is at a particular point in its cycle. The tradition observed this before modern science had language for it. Fasting on Ekadashi was recorded as making the body lighter and the mind clearer. That observation is in texts thousands of years old. Try it once and notice for yourself.

*What to do today:* The graduated fast: strictest is nirjala (nothing, not even water). Standard is no grains or legumes — fruit, dairy, root vegetables, certain permitted grains. Lightest is one meal. Choose the level you can genuinely hold. The discipline is the practice — not the suffering.

**THE FOOD**
Ekadashi fasting food is its own cuisine, developed across centuries of creative constraint: sabudana (tapioca) khichdi with peanuts and cumin; makhana (lotus seeds) stir-fried in ghee with rock salt; kuttu (buckwheat) chapati; singhare ki puri (water chestnut flour flatbread); fruit, milk, yogurt. The fast breaks the next morning after sunrise with regular food, beginning with something light.

## 🌑 AMAVASYA — THE NEW MOON (MONTHLY)
*15th tithi of Krishna Paksha, every month*
*The darkest night. Ancestral. Inward. For remembering.*

**STANDARD AMAVASYA — EVE**
> Tomorrow is Amavasya — the new moon, the darkest night of the month. The tradition associates this day with ancestors. *Pitru Tarpan* — the water offering to those who came before you. If your parents or grandparents have passed: tomorrow is when to remember them, formally. Light a lamp for them. Say their names out loud. If they are still alive: call them.

**MAHALAYA AMAVASYA (September/October — the most important)**
> Tomorrow is Mahalaya Amavasya — the most significant new moon of the year for ancestor remembrance. It ends the fifteen-day period of Pitru Paksha, the fortnight of ancestors. If you observe tarpan at any point in the year, this is the day. Say the names of your grandparents. Say the names of their parents. Three generations. Water poured through open hands, facing the sun. The river of the living flowing back toward the river of the dead.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Amavasya — the new moon — is the darkest night of the month. Where Purnima is outward, bright, and social, Amavasya is inward, dark, and ancestral. The tradition associates it with the *pitrus* — the ancestors, the ones who came before.

*The mythology:* The *pitrus* occupy a specific cosmological position in Hindu thought — neither fully in the world of the living nor fully in the realm beyond. They require acknowledgment and offerings to continue their journey. *Pitru Tarpan* is that offering: water poured through the hands, toward the sun, accompanied by the names of three generations of ancestors. The water is the vehicle. The names are the prayer.

*The history:* Ancestor veneration is among the most universal human practices — it appears in Chinese, Japanese, African, Native American, and most other traditions alongside the Hindu one. The specific mechanism differs, but the underlying recognition is the same: the living are continuous with those who came before, and that continuity requires acknowledgment. You carry their traits, their unfinished work, their gifts, their wounds. Tarpan is the ritual recognition of that fact.

*What to do today:* Say the names of your grandparents. If you know their parents' names, say those too. Pour water from a glass toward the light — any light, any water, any direction that feels like it points away from here and toward wherever they are. The specific ritual can be learned later. The intention is enough to start.

## 🌊 GURU PURNIMA
*~July | Purnima of Ashadha*
*The full moon of gratitude.*

**ADVANCE (7 days out)**
> Guru Purnima is in a week — the full moon of Ashadha, set aside to honor whoever has helped you see more clearly. Not just formal teachers. A mentor who believed in you before you believed in yourself. A book that arrived at exactly the right moment. A conversation that changed the direction you were walking. The tradition says: acknowledge that you didn't get here alone.

**EVE**
> Tomorrow: Guru Purnima. The word *guru* means one who dispels darkness — *gu* (darkness) *ru* (that which removes). Not a title. A function. Think tonight about where you were in the dark, and what or who brought light.

**MORNING**
> Guru Purnima. Full moon of gratitude. One message to send today — to whoever deserves it.

**DEEP DIVE**

*What it actually is:* Guru Purnima falls on the full moon of Ashadha, honoring Vyasa — the sage who compiled and organized the Vedas into their current form, and who is considered the Adi Guru, the first teacher. It is observed by Hindus, Buddhists (as the day of the Buddha's first teaching), and Jains. One full moon, three traditions, the same underlying acknowledgment.

*The mythology:* Vyasa is the author of the Mahabharata, the compiler of the Vedas, and the grandfather of both the Pandavas and Kauravas whose war he wrote about. The tradition holds that without Vyasa, the knowledge of the Vedic age would have been lost — he organized what existed into something transmissible. Guru Purnima honors the function he represents: the human link in the chain of knowledge.

*The history:* In the yogic tradition, Guru Purnima is the day Shiva became the Adi Guru — the first teacher — transmitting the knowledge of yoga to the seven sages (Saptarishis) on the banks of the Kantisarovar lake in the Himalayas. This happened, the tradition says, after he sat in meditation so still that onlookers eventually realized he was not going to move, and those who wanted what he had came and sat with him.

*What to do today:* Send one message. Call one person. The tradition doesn't require elaborate ritual — it requires acknowledgment. Tell someone that they changed how you see. That's the whole festival.

## THE PAKSHA TRANSITION

Twice a month, the moon crosses its midpoint — from waxing to waning, and from waning to new. These transitions are marked in the panchang but explained nowhere. This app explains them.

**SHUKLA TO KRISHNA (Full moon → waning)**
> The moon is full tonight — and from tomorrow, it begins to diminish. Shukla Paksha ends, Krishna Paksha begins. In the tradition: the waxing moon is for beginning things, the waning moon is for completing them. The next two weeks: finish what you started.

**KRISHNA TO SHUKLA (New moon → waxing)**
> The new moon was last night. From today: Shukla Paksha, the bright fortnight, the moon building back toward full. Traditionally the time for new beginnings, for planting, for starting ventures. What are you beginning in this fortnight?

---

## THE NAMED TITHIS

**CHATURTHI (4th tithi) — Ganesha's day, twice monthly**
> Chaturthi today — Ganesha's tithi, arriving twice a month, every month. If something has felt stuck: this is when you ask the remover of obstacles for some help. A small puja, or just the acknowledgment that you've been working around something instead of through it.

**PRADOSH (13th tithi) — Shiva's evening, twice monthly**
> Pradosh this evening — the thirteenth tithi, Shiva's window. The tradition says the hour around sunset tonight is especially powerful for Shiva puja. The name means "twilight" — the specific light between day and night where the ordinary rules become more flexible. Light something at dusk if you can.

**ASHTAMI (8th tithi) — Durga's midpoint**
> Ashtami today — the midpoint of the fortnight, Durga's tithi. Eight is the number of directions plus the center — the totality of space. Ashtami arrives in both fortnights, reminding the calendar that power doesn't wait for the full moon or the new moon. It arrives at the center.

## BESTU VARAS — GUJARATI NEW YEAR
*Kartika Shukla Pratipada — the morning after Diwali*

**MORNING**
> Saal Mubarak. The Gujarati new year begins not in January, not at the spring equinox, but the morning after Diwali — when the darkness is over and the light has held. The Vikram Samvat turns today. [YEAR] begins now. What are you beginning?

**DEEP DIVE**
Bestu Varas — literally "new year" in Gujarati — is the first day of the Vikram Samvat year as reckoned in Gujarat. While North India begins the Samvat in Chaitra (spring), the Gujarati tradition uses the Kartikadi calendar: the year begins on Kartika Shukla Pratipada, the day after Diwali's Amavasya.

This means the Gujarati Samvat year number runs one behind the North Indian count between Chaitra and Kartik — something that catches every Gujarati panchang designer eventually. But more meaningfully, it means the Gujarati new year begins at the darkest point of the year and immediately after — when the lamps have been lit, the darkness has been confronted, and something new becomes possible.

The tradition of Chopda Puja on Bestu Varas is worth knowing: merchants and business people open new account books on this day and have them blessed. The first entry in the new ledger is often *Shri Ganeshaya Namah* — a dedication to Ganesha at the start of a new year of accounts. The bookkeeping year turns with the calendar year.

Reference the five writing rules: (1) earn unfamiliar words through image not definition, (2) specific beats vague always, (3) emotional beat comes last, (4) bridge the ancient and the observable without overclaiming, (5) end at human scale with a specific action.

---

## TONE GUARDRAILS — NEVER

- "On this auspicious occasion"
- "Seek blessings" without explaining whose and why
- "It is believed that" — write what the tradition says and let the reader decide
- "Whether or not you believe in..." — defensive about belief
- Sanskrit terms in parenthetical definitions — earn them with the next phrase
- Passive constructions that distance the reader from the tradition ("the goddess is worshipped")
- Superlatives without support ("the most important," "the most sacred") unless the tradition actually designates it that way

## TONE GUARDRAILS — ALWAYS

- End at human scale: a specific action, a person to call, a thing to make
- Assume the reader is intelligent and curious, not ignorant and needing correction
- Write the mythology as story, not as doctrine
- Acknowledge regional variation — the tradition is not monolithic
- The food belongs in every entry — find it

---

## THE DEEP DIVE STRUCTURE (5 paragraphs, always)

1. **What it actually is** — stripped of assumption, for someone who has never heard of it
2. **The mythology** — the story, told as story not doctrine
3. **The history** — how it has traveled and changed
4. **The regional variation** — where it looks different, and why
5. **What to do today** — wherever you are, whatever your level of observance
